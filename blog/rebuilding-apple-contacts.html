Building Recall

I Think I'm Just
Rebuilding Apple
Contacts.

Paul Barbera  ·  June 2026  ·  4 min read

I've been building Recall part-time. Quietly. Scratching an itch. And somewhere in the last few months it hit me — I'm not really making a memory app. I'm rebuilding the contacts app that's been sitting on my phone since 2007.

The honest truth is I started this for me. I wanted a contacts app that worked the way my brain actually works — context first, name second. Tags, hashtags, the ability to pull up everyone from Mia's school in two taps. Not a corporate CRM. Not LinkedIn. Just my contacts, but smarter.

What I didn't expect was that the process itself would be the reward. Having complete control over an interface — without trying to make it sound bigger than it is — has been genuinely fun. I'd forgotten what that felt like.

Two groups keep showing up.

As I've iterated, two niches have really lit up.

Parents. You can name every kid in your child's class, including their dogs, including their birthday party themes — but you cannot for the life of you remember any of the other parents' names. There's actually a word for this — teknonymy — and I wrote a whole separate piece on it. The short version: it's universal, it's ancient, and your phone has no way to handle it.

Dog people. Same problem, different species. You know every dog at the park. Bear. Luna. Murphy. Their owners? Total blanks. You greet the dog, nod at the human, hope they don't notice.

Both groups need the same thing: store people by the thing that actually anchors them in your memory.

The boring stuff matters too.

Lately I've added the maintenance features the app needed to actually be useful day to day:

  • Merge duplicates — because everyone's contacts list is a graveyard of "John," "John 2," "John (iCloud old)."
  • BCC-style group texting — send one message to a group without creating a chaotic group thread. Still a bit clunky. Getting there.
  • Better tagging, faster search, smarter empty states.

None of it is sexy. All of it is necessary.

Then it hit me.

Apple's Contacts app started life as Address Book in Mac OS X 10.2 Jaguar — released August 2002. That's twenty-four years ago. It jumped to iPhone in 2007, and in the nearly two decades since, the core experience has barely changed. Same first-name-last-name fields. Same flat alphabetical list. Same logic that assumed our social world was a Rolodex.

But the way we know people now is completely different. We know them by their kids. Their dogs. The Slack we met them in. The conference. The school gate. The dog park. The neighbour two doors down whose name we never caught but whose parcels we sign for.

Our brains organise people by context. Apple Contacts organises them by alphabet.

That gap is what I've been quietly filling in.

"I thought I was making a memory app. Turns out I'm making the contacts app Apple forgot to build."

So here's the question.

The pitch I've been using is memory — remember anyone, by anything. And it works. But I'm starting to think the truer pitch is simpler:

A better contacts app.

More powerful. Built for how we actually know people in 2026, not how we knew them in 2002. Still uses your iCloud contacts under the hood, so nothing to migrate. Just better, on top.

I'd genuinely love to know what you think.
